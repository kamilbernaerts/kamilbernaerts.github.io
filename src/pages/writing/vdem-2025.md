Although dramatic both in style and in scope, the democratic erosion unfolding right before our eyes in the US under President Trump is unfortunately not a unique case. It is quite established by now within political science that worldwide democracy is in retreat and under increasing pressure. In other words, we are in the midst of a [third wave of autocratisation](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/13510347.2019.1582029). To measure just how widespread and steep this wave is, most political scientists rely on the widely trusted V-Dem dataset. [V-Dem](https://www.v-dem.net) is a research centre connected to the University of Gothenburg in Sweden which collects macro-level data using expert surveys on an impressive battery of democracy and democracy-related indicators. On March 17th, they published their [annual report on the state of worldwide democracy](https://www.v-dem.net/news/press-release-democratic-backsliding-reaches-western-democracies-with-us-decline-unprecedented/), and the accompanying 2025 update of their publicly available dataset. And it can, without exaggeration, be seen as dramatic. The third wave of autocratisation shows absolutely no sign of slowing down, to the contrary: not only has the global state of democracy fallen back to the level of 1978, there are also an unprecedented high number of countries autocratising and autocratisation has now reached all regions of the world. It is clear that democracy is now facing existential threats around the world.

Based on V-Dem's categorisation of regimes, the world in 2025 is summarised in *Figure 1*. Dark red and orange countries are respectively **closed** and **electoral autocracies**. Both of these regime types do not have free and fair elections (although electoral autocracies do allow multiparty elections) nor do they protect fundamental democratic rights. Light-blue and dark blue countries are respectively **electoral** and **liberal democracies**. Both have free and fair elections, and liberal democracies also have strong protections of fundamental rights and liberties ([see here for more information on this index](https://ourworldindata.org/regimes-of-the-world-data)). When we count the number of citizens as a share of the world population in each of these broad categories, it is quite telling already that only 7% of world citizens live in liberal democracies, while 28% live in closed autocracies. Even more striking, more citizens now live in closed autocracies than in all democracies combined (26%). For the second year in a row, liberal democracy is now the least common regime type in the world. When we take a broader distinction between all autocracies and all democracies, almost three out of four citizens (74%) live in autocratic states.

Compared over time, this is a clear downward trend relative to the peak of the most recent wave of democratisation (see *Figure 2* and *Figure 3*): around 2005, approximately 17% of world citizens lived in a liberal democracy — a figure that has since more than halved to just **7%** today, largely due to the recent democratic deterioration in the United States. When we compare countries over time, it is clear that liberal democratic countries are disappearing quite quickly (see Figure 2). From the peak of 45 liberal democratic countries in 2010, only 31 remained in 2025. As we come from a historically unique period of democratic triumph, this means that the downward trend does not directly push all liberal democracies to closed autocracy. Most gains are to be found in electoral democracies (which are indeed increasing in number). Yet, this autocratising trend does not limit itself to democratic regimes: in countries which were already autocratic, we also see a clear trend of further autocratisation.

If we go further back in time, it is clear that, luckily, we are still far away from the heydays of autocracy in the pre-1950s. Yet, V-Dem writes that almost all the gains of the third wave of democratisation (starting with Portugal's Carnation Revolution in 1974) are now eradicated. Furthermore, we can also see an acceleration in the speed and intensity of autocratisation. In 2025 no less than 41% of the world population lived in autocratising countries. And as prior years saw that autocratisation dynamics were still rare in the West, it is striking that in 2025 it was Western Europe and North America which showed the most intense decline in average levels of democracy.

## What aspects of democracy are under attack?

Beyond broad regime classifications, V-Dem's data allow us to identify precisely which democratic components are deteriorating. The pattern is strikingly consistent across autocratising countries. Freedom of expression, and media freedom in particular, is the most universally attacked aspect of democracy. Government censorship of the media is deteriorating in 32 of the 44 current autocratising countries (73%), making it by far the most common instrument of autocratisers worldwide. When we compare the situation in 2025 to that in 2000, the reversal is staggering: 25 years ago, 52 countries were improving on freedom of expression while only five were declining; today, 44 countries are declining and only eleven improving. Freedom of academic and cultural expression, media self-censorship, and harassment of journalists are all showing broad deterioration.

What is more, civil society repression has surged dramatically and now constitutes the second most common autocratising tactic. Given the great importance of a strong civil society in vibrant democracies, this is deeply worrying. It affects 30 of the 44 autocratising countries (68%), up from 22 (49%) just one year prior — an increase of 19 percentage points in a single year. This means that organisations working on human rights, environmental protection, minority rights, or political opposition are facing government-imposed restrictions in over two thirds of all autocratising countries simultaneously. The third major area of concern is the erosion of checks and balances: the institutions that constrain executive power. Legislative constraints on the executive, judicial independence, and compliance with court rulings are all declining in a large and growing number of countries. In the United States, legislative constraints on the executive dropped by no less than one-third of their value in a single year — the largest such drop in American history going back to 1789 — reaching the lowest level in over 100 years. The message from V-Dem's data is clear: autocratisers systematically silence the press, strangle civil society, and concentrate executive power. On the other hand, the integrity of free elections remains mostly intact — for now.

## Washington is leading, but in the wrong way

Autocratisation now affects every region of the world, though its character and intensity vary. Western Europe and North America, traditionally the home territory of global democracy, now registers the most notable population-weighted decline in average democracy levels, driven primarily by the United States. Under President Trump's second term, this country stands out as the most dramatic and globally consequential case (V-Dem devoted a whole section in their annual report to this case). Not only is the US still one of the most powerful and influential nations in the world, it is also of great symbolic value that democracy is eroding there, as it is often seen as one of the oldest and most respected democracies in history.

In a single year, the US fell from 20th to 51st place in V-Dem's global democracy ranking, with its Liberal Democracy Index score dropping from 0.79 to 0.57. V-Dem describes this as the largest single-year decline in American history going back to 1789. The current level of democracy in the US is now equivalent to that of 1965 — the year of the Voting Rights Act and the symbolic starting point of a fully functioning American democracy. The driving force is what V-Dem calls "the most rapid executive aggrandizement in modern history": a systematic concentration of power in the presidency at the expense of Congress, the judiciary, the press, civil society, and academia. Legislative constraints on the executive fell by one third in a single year. Freedom of expression, civil rights, and judicial independence have all deteriorated sharply. To put this in context: it took Hungary's Viktor Orbán four years to achieve a comparable drop, Serbia's Aleksandar Vučić eight years, and both India's Narendra Modi and Turkey's Recep Tayyip Erdoğan approximately a decade. Trump managed it in one.

Yet the US is not an isolated case among established democracies. The United Kingdom was newly identified as an autocratiser beginning in 2020, driven largely by a sharp decline in freedom of expression and media freedoms. Italy joined the list in 2021. Croatia, Slovakia, and Slovenia were all classified as new autocratisers in 2025. Hungary has been deepening its autocratic trajectory since 2009 and is now a firmly consolidated electoral autocracy. India — the world's most populous democracy until recently — has been undergoing slow but systematic democratic erosion since 2008, driven by attacks on press freedom, civil society, and the independence of institutions. In short, liberal democracies that were once thought to be immune to the autocratising wave have turned out to be equally vulnerable.

Against this backdrop, a small number of democratising cases provide cautious reason for hope. Poland's U-turn — following the 2023 elections that brought Donald Tusk's broad coalition to power after years of democratic erosion under the Law and Justice party — is the most notable European example. Poland has so far managed to reverse autocratisation without a democratic breakdown, though it remains below its 2015 democracy levels. Brazil similarly halted and reversed its autocratising trajectory under Lula, aided in part by Bolsonaro's criminal conviction and subsequent bar from office — a great and important win for the integrity of the *trias politica* and the resilience of democratic institutions. In sub-Saharan Africa, both Botswana and Mauritius achieved meaningful democratic progress through peaceful electoral transfers of power in 2024. These "U-turn" cases demonstrate that democratic backsliding is not irreversible, and that elections remain pivotal windows of opportunity. Yet, at the same time there was no single stand-alone case of democratisation to be observed (in contrast to countries rebounding from a period of autocratisation), suggesting that the global attraction of democracy has faded.

## At the crest of the wave?

Despite this overwhelming doom, one potential cause for optimism might be that, however dire the situation looks, it is possible that we are at the crest of the wave and that the situation might start improving again. V-Dem itself cautiously notes a small decline in the number of simultaneously autocratising countries — from a historical peak of 50 in 2021 to 44 in 2025 — raising the question of whether the "third wave of autocratisation" may be cresting.

However, several important caveats must be made before concluding that the tide is actually turning. First, 10 new autocratisers were identified in 2025 while 11 episodes ended, and several of those endings represent not democratic recovery but the stabilisation of fully entrenched autocratic regimes (as with Russia and Turkey). Second, V-Dem's methodology is deliberately conservative and back-dates episode starts; the actual 2025 count may yet increase as additional data becomes available. Third, and most critically: the overall scale of democratic losses is without historical precedent. A [recent paper by Staffan Lindberg](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/13510347.2026.2615134), chief editor of the report, demonstrates that the current wave of autocratisation is worse than the 1930s in terms of length, scope, and magnitude. The number of people now living in autocratising countries (41%) has never been higher in recorded history. Even if the number of autocratising countries were to stabilise, we would still be living with the accumulated damage of decades of democratic erosion. The wave may or may not be cresting; but we are, without question, facing the full force of the autocratising tsunami.

## A wake-up call

For those of us fortunate enough to live in stable liberal democracies such as Belgium, it may be tempting to treat these trends as distant concerns. That would be a serious mistake, and for reasons that go well beyond simple solidarity with citizens in less fortunate countries. After all, the current wave of autocratisation is unprecedented not only in its scale and intensity, but also in terms of *who* is autocratising. For the first time, the major drivers of democratic decline include some of the world's most economically and geopolitically powerful nations. Four of the five most populous countries in the world are now autocracies (China, India, Indonesia, and Pakistan), and the United States — by far the world's largest economy and the historical gravity point of the post-war liberal order — is rapidly deteriorating on virtually every democratic dimension. By GDP-weighted averages, the level of democracy in the world is now at its lowest in over 50 years, having declined by more than 36% from its peak in 2000.

This matters because the institutions that shape our global order are now increasingly populated and actively influenced by autocratic or rapidly autocratising states. International norms around human rights, press freedom, the rule of law, and democratic legitimacy are under sustained and coordinated pressure from within the very bodies that were designed to uphold them. Trade deals, foreign policy alignments, aid conditionality, and multilateral climate or security agreements are all being reshaped by states whose domestic political logic is becoming more and more autocratic. Understanding this context is not optional for us in liberal democracies: it is essential for interpreting the international events, diplomatic tensions, and policy shifts that increasingly define our political present. Furthermore, it should make us realise how precarious and unique liberal democracy has become.

After all, as shown above, the autocratising tsunami has also made landfall in established liberal democracies. The United States, the United Kingdom, and Italy — once full liberal democracies and long-standing members of the Western democratic culture — are now classified as countries in active autocratisation episodes. Liberal institutions everywhere are under pressure.

If anything, this report above all has shown that democracy is fragile and should never be taken for granted. This should be a wake-up call for protecting democracy abroad, but also here at home. Although Belgium is still characterised as a liberal democracy, we should never let our guard down — and we should appreciate the uniqueness of where we live.
